Grad operates on a scalar field $f(x, y)$ to produce a vector field indicating the magnitude and direction of the steepest gradient at each point. It is easy to remember that grad produces a vector field, because it is necessary for the gradient to have a direction once there is more than one dimension involved. It must act on a scalar field because it doesn't make much sense for a vector field to have a gradient.

The surface shown on the right is a scalar function $f$ of two independent variables, $x$ and $y$. Note that it is a 2D scalar field — the third dimension is just being used to display the value of the function.

Beneath it the gradient of the function $\nabla f$ is shown. By changing the coefficient $a$, the gradient of the function can be increased and decreased in certain directions. There are also several functions to choose from.

The idea extends to three dimensions in the same way. For example, if you were to plot the magnitude of the Sun's gravitational field in 3D space around the Sun, the gradient of this field would give vectors that point to the centre of the Sun at every point.

$$ \Omega_B - \Omega_A = \int _A ^B \nabla \Omega \cdot \mathbf{ dl }$$


For a given vector field, if $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{ dl }$ is an exact differential, $\mathbf{A}$ is a conservative field. Then, $\mathbf{A}$ is the gradient function of some scalar function $\Omega$. That is, $\mathbf{A} = \nabla {\Omega}$. We can relate $\Omega$ and $\nabla \Omega$ by the equation below: $$ \int _A ^B \nabla \Omega \cdot \mathbf{ dl } = \Omega_B - \Omega_A$$ From this equation above, we can see that the integral is independent of the path that one takes from A to B, for it is determined by only the values from the parent function $\Omega_B - \Omega_A$. This is a generalisation of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

Visualisation Guide
  1. On the right, you can see a gradient field and its scalar parent function. Change the value of the parameter $a$, and observe the change in $\nabla \Omega$ and $\Omega$.
  2. Note that as the magnitude of $a$ increases, the length of the arrows in $\nabla \Omega$ increases and the contour of the parent function $\Omega$ becomes steeper as well. Hence, the gradient vector increases in magnitude as well.
  3. All gradient fields shown in this suite are conservative. This means that the line integral along the field, $\int \nabla \Omega \cdot \mathbf{ dl }$ is path independent. Move the path of the ball from Point A to Point B along 2 different paths, Path 1 and Path 2.
  4. Observe their function values. Note that along the path, the value of $\Omega$ may be different. However, when the balls reach Point B, their function values are equal.
  5. This means that the line integral $\int _A ^B \nabla \Omega \cdot \mathbf{ dl }$ is determined only by the value of $\Omega$ at the limits, $\Omega_B - \Omega_A$.
  6. There are a few other functions for you to play around with. Click the dropdown menu to find out.

What is divergence?

The divergence of a field quantifies how much a field varies in the direction of the field lines. A field is said to be diverging if the field strength changes as you step along field lines. On an infinitesimal level, this may be interpreted as 'sources' of field strength 'creating' or 'destroying' field lines. It may be calculated using the $\displaystyle \nabla $ operator on a vector field to generate a scalar field. The Visualisation on the right shows an infinitesimal section of a surface (the y axis). The arrows show the direction of the field lines while their thickness and colour represent the field strength. Use the $\displaystyle \nabla $ slider to understand the difference between positive and negative divergence.

Divergence may also occur if there are sources of field lines (e.g. monopoles) that increase/decrease the line density of a field, as shown by the figure to the right. The source of divergence adds field lines whereas the sink takes them away. Hence the source has a positive divergence and the sink has a negative divergence.

Flux - the flow of field lines

We often call $\mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S}$ the flux of field $\mathbf{F}$ perpendicular through a surface element $dS$. In this visualisation, the green line can be thought as taking the side view of the surface element.

The net flux through any surface $S$ is given by the integral: $$\iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S}$$ By considering a closed surface, the Divergence Theorem relates the net flux to the divergence of the field:
$\unicode{x222F}_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} dV$

An example of the Divergence Theorem is Maxwell's Equation.

Stokes' Theorem states:
$\displaystyle \oint_{l} \mathbf{A.dl} =$ $\displaystyle \iint_{S} \nabla \times \mathbf {A} \cdot d\mathbf{S}$
i.e. the closed loop integral of a vector field is equal to the sum of all the curl contained in the surface enclosed by the loop.

This is demonstrated in the visualisation on the right, where the components of $\displaystyle \mathbf{A.dl}$ as you go around the loop are shown in the plot at the bottom. The contributions are positive if the field and dl are pointing in the same direction.

When there is curl inside the loop then the overall loop integral is non-zero, since on all parts of the loop, the contributions to the integral from the curl source have the same sign; however when the curl source is outside of the loop, then the contributions cancel out and the net integral is zero.

This also demonstrates that the curl of a conservative field is zero, since any loop integral in a conservative field is also zero.

You can change the shape of the loop to see that this is true for any type of closed loop.
